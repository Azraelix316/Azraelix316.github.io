Step 4: Write!

The social changes of democratization were a major factor to why social changes were significantly influenced by the government and not completely influenced by the government.
One of the examples of successful democratization is the Wild Lily movement in 1990, a student led movement triggered by the funeral of the self-immolation of a journalist in Taiwan that demanded faster steps to democracy. The Wild Lily movement was successful and forced Lee Teng Hui to accept their demands, such as dissolving the national assembly and providing a timetable for clear steps towards a true democracy. The Wild Lily Movement shows how large, mass movements of people can place pressure on the government and demand social change towards democratization. Democratization is one of the most significant social changes inside a nation, a complete change of political structure. This is relevant because the Wild Lily movement shows how the people directly contribute towards democratization and thus social change - the people led this movement solely of their own volition, which shows how the government sometimes does not have full control of social changes.
However, suppression of people's movements also exists. One of the most extreme cases was the Tiananmen Square Incident in China in 1989, where over 300 civilians were shot down by the CCP military forces trying to clear Tiananmen square, filled with 2 million people. Afterwards, the CCP tightened censorship and prevented further steps towards democratization. Tiananmen square was the turning point for China, and the actions that the CCP prevented more people's movements in the future. As such, in China there has not been much social change since that period - there have no further mass demonstrations due to the prevention of people's movements. This is relevant because it shows us how when the people are unable to gather and unable to demonstrate, social changes are much more difficult to create. While the government can suppress and prevent changes, many actual social changes result from the people.
Furthermore, the Cultural Revolution is another example of how social changes must result from the people's actions. In the 1960s, Mao had lost power and needed to reclaim his status in the CCP. To do so, he created the four olds (old habits, customs, ideas, and structures) and utilized propaganda in order to create the Red Guards, his main troops during the Cultural Revolution. Mainly students, they idolized Mao and created chaos through China. The Cultural Revolution and the Red Guards show how even political leaders need people in order to change political structures. Mao needed to have actual people to rise back to the top of the CCP and put pressure on reformists to put him back in power. The Red Guards show how powerful people acting in large masses can be and how much change they can create - many intellectuals left China and China's reforms were mostly removed and reverted to Maoist/communist ideals. This is relevant because it shows how social change is physically executed by people. While Mao manipulated the people, the Cultural Revolution shows how governments and political leaders alone cannot create cultural change, as culture is controlled by the people's ideas. Only by manipulating the people can they truly create social change.

Social changes brought by economic policies are a highly significant factor to why the government significantly influenced social changes. While the government alone cannot create cultural change, they can create economic policies that lead to economic change and improve living conditions, such as in the case of Taiwan's Kaohsiung Export Processing Zone. The government created an export processing zone that was solely for export, and had incentives for foreign investors. This caused the U.S and Japan to outsource their work to Taiwan, and significantly improved Taiwan's economy leading to the development of "Taiwan's Economic Miracle" where Taiwan was the fastest growing economy for 2 decades. The S.E.Z shows us how the government can contribute heavily to economic growth and improve living conditions for its population. The government was solely responsible for the creation of the economic zone and the incentives that allowed for so many factories and businesses to prosper, which shows us how economic growth in Taiwan was triggered by the government's actions. As such, many of the improvements and changes to living conditions were caused by the Taiwanese government. Furthermore, governments can also completely control the economy, such as with the 5-year plans first instituted in China in 1952. The 1st 5-year plan led to many quantifiable improvements - coal production went from 65 million tons to 130 million tons a year, steel and iron production tripled, and railways increased significantly. The second 5-yea plan had goals set too high and led to mass starvation in China. The 5-year plans show us how governments have immense control over their economic policies which have a massive effect on the population. While the 1st 5-year plan led to many improvements for the people of China, the Great Leap Forward was unsuccessful and killed millions. The 5-year plans show how governments have great control over the living conditions of their population via economic policies. Living conditions, an example of social changes, can be greatly influenced by the government for better or for worse.
Another example of the influence of governments on social changes and economic policies is Deng's conversion of China from a communist country to one of "socialism with Chinese Characteristics", or a market-based country. Deng's conversion of China to a more capitalist economy shows how economic policies by governments can alter the identity of a country - a key part of the identity of a country is its economic structure and ideology. Deng's conversion of China's economic structure is relevant because it shows how governments can control a countries' identity. Economic changes brought by governments can create massive social changes such as converting the entire economic structure of a nation.

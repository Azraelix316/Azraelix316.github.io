Song Agri Techniques

China developed many methods in order to increase agricultural production, such as irrigation, terrace farming, and crop rotation. These methods helped its population explode and made farming very efficient, and would be transferred to the European states.
This helped China legitimize their rule is by increasing prosperity, since a prosperous nation is one that is less likely to have rebellions. China was able to be stable during the Song dynasty since there was a food surplus to power population growth without starvation. This stability allowed the Song dynasty to maintain its rule and their Mandate Of Heaven
Eventually, these techniques would spread throughout the SIlk Road, and by not having to live hand to mouth, Commercialization occured on the global scale, improving the entire world economy.

Filial Piety

Respecting one's elders: a fundamental part of Chinese and Confucianist society. Filial piety helped reinforce the 5 Relationships and the clear social structure of Chinese society, making it even more difficult to rebel and leave one's social class as it acts as a cultural tie to maintain social hierarchies.

5 Relationships

The 5 key relationships: husband-wife, peer-peer, ruler-subject, father-son, elder-younger sibling, were core relationships and had clear superior inferior interactions in Confucianism. This helped create a social hierarchy, making it easier for a ruler to govern. Another effect is in gender roles, where it developed and created a patriarchal society which can be seen through acts such as Foot Binding.

Meritocratic Examinations

Meritocratic examinations that anyone could take, leading to the creation of a scholar-official bureaucratic class. However, it was still unbalanced and unfair due to tutoring, much like the SAT and other "standardized" exams today, rich families could afford the tutors necessary, but sometimes poor towns would sponsor someone. By allowing limited social ability, rulers gave commoners a way to improve their status keeping commoners more accepting of their lowered social status (they "deserved" it). However, the prohibitive cost also served to still keep commoners out of higher social levels, and helped keep the rulers in power.

Changes across Dynasties

The most important thing was that in BOTH the Ming and Song dynasties, Confucianism was a continuity. This continuity is important because it shows us how even in the face of the Mongols, China itself changed little to accommodate for them, culturally and socially, and the major changes were economic and political. The "heart" of China stayed the same.

Yam System

The securing of the Mongol empire's trade routes, leading to the Pax Mongolica. These relays made it much safer and cheaper for trade to occur, and these relays helped with communication as well and governance over their massive empire. The relays did not govern trade, instead they secured the trade routes and only taxed merchants using their services slightly - by not controlling trade, and only taxing merchants, trade still occurred and the increased safety overweighed the cost.
Thus, the Yam System, by securing trade routes, helped expand the use of trade networks and due to the size of the Mongol empire, helped facilitate cross-regional interactions.

Pax Mongolica

The Pax Mongolica was a period of peace under the Mongols in which the Mongols secured the Silk Road with the use of the Yam system. These relays helped secure the trade routes and made it much safer and cheaper to trade. Additionally, the expansion of the Mongol empire meant that many regions like Russia, China, Persia, and parts of Europe were now interconnected. Under the Pax Mongolica, trade was encouraged and incentivized, leading to the growth of trade across the giant Mongol empire.
The state encouragement of trade and the deliberate securing of trade routes led to expanded networks of exchange.

This peace was a MASSIVE change, due to the immense scale that the Mongols aimed to conduct trade at - never before had previously disconnected regions interacted at such a large scale. Before, ideas, culture, goods, and technology only moved through a single region, but after the Mongols connected these regions (Persia, Europe, china, etc), ideas, technology, etc. moved across regions (examples could be gunpowder moving further just China to Europe, compass, etc.)

Tribute System

China gained immense wealth by forcing other nations to pay tribute to them, in goods or money (goods like Champa Rice were accepted as well). They mainly had three major tributary states: Vietnam, Japan, and Korea.
By forcing other states to pay tribute, China was able to maintain power and prevent the other states from accumulating too much wealth. The tribute system was based on the idea that China is the cultural, economic, and technological leader of Asia, and as long as tribute states kept paying tribute, that idea was perpetuated and legitimized China’s rule.
An effect of this was that China would sinicize other nations and cause them to change.
Korea would basically become a Chinese clone, adopting Confucianism entirely, and Neo-Confucianism was adopted by Japan and Korea.
The Chinese Writing system was adopted by all three as well.
Furthermore, in Japan and Korea (Vietnam resisted), the role of women also shifted due to Confucianism, and these nations became more patriarchal.

Sub-Saharan Technological Innovations

Camels

Camels were the primary beast of burden, and were necessary to traverse the Saharan desert due to their capacity to stay alive and not die, unlike horses. Without camels, it would be impossible to develop the sub-saharan trade networks to the scale they existed at, as large-scale trade would not be possible.

Camel-Saddles

Camels only became useful after the invention of camel-saddles, facilitating trade in the Sahara as they could finally be used as true beasts of burden to carry loads, not just people. Without the camel-saddle and the camel, large transactions, especially transactions of SALT, which was vital to the Saharan economy.

Guides & Knowledge

Knowledge of the Saharan environment and the existence of guides was also tremendously important, for example in the case of Ibn Battuta, we know that guides were common and widespread. Due to the harsh nature of the Sahara, it would have been suicide to attempt a crossing without knowledge of oasises and other intermediate destinations, and as knowledge was accumulated, it led to the growth of trade networks as merchants found it more safe to conduct business.

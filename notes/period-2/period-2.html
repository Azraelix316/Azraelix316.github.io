Imperial Examinations

These imperial examinations continued from the past, from period 1 (A continuity!). The core governing system of the Qing was still Confucianism, which obviously continued to have similar benefits.

Quote from other note:

These were meritocratic examinations that anyone could take, leading to the creation of a scholar-official bureaucratic class. However, it was still unbalanced and unfair due to tutoring, much like the SAT and other "standardized" exams today, rich families could afford the tutors necessary, but sometimes poor towns would sponsor someone. By allowing limited social ability, rulers gave commoners a way to improve their status keeping commoners more accepting of their lowered social status (they "deserved" it). However, the prohibitive cost also served to still keep commoners out of higher social levels, and helped keep the rulers in power.

Ghulam

The Ghulam system was a system in which prisoners of war would be enslaved and used as slave soldiers. This provided an elite military force, and similar to the Mamluks, while they were slaves they were still paid highly and treated well in order to prevent rebellion.
They served a similar role to Mamluks and Janissaries, where like in the Devisherme system these soldiers would be indoctrinated in loyalty to the Shah.
This gave the Safavid empire one of the first examples of a trained military force, and helped maintain power by keeping the military under direct control of the ruler, and ensured that the military wasn't loyal to aristocratic families

The Iltizam, tax farming under the Ottomans, where the rights to taxing a certain piece of land were auctioned off and sold to the highest bidder.
The Iltizam allowed the Ottomans to gain money to fund their extensive military campaigns, but eventually the ottomans would have to cut back and find other ways to raise cash, as slowly they lost more and more revenue
Similar to Zamindars, sometimes sacrifices had to be made for effective taxation and economic growth.

Zamindars

Extremely similar to the Mansabdars, but they were hereditary and only served as intermediaries between the people and the ruler. Similar to the Mansabdars, they became corrupt and splintered the Mughal empire.
Essentially they served the same role as the Mansabdars in terms of tax collection, allowing the empire to be able to collect taxes over their huge territory.
Thus, they show that the Mughals had to sacrifice some amount of centralized control for effective taxation policies, similar to the Yasak System where furs were taken as tax but natives had different methods of tax collection.

One of the other great powers, only behind the Qing empire (but definitely extremely powerful militarily). Due to its amazing geographic position, they acted as middlemen for Silk Road trade to Italian city states in the Mediterranean. Their markups were what caused Portugal and Spain to seek new sea routes to China for their ever-increasing desire of Chinese luxury goods.
They would utilize a strong administration and military system to completely dominate all their neighbors, especially the Safavid empire in the Battle of Chaldiran as a Sunni empire.

Ottoman Empire

Qing Empire

The Qing empire during this time period was still undeniably the single most influential empire in the world, evidenced by the entire world economy being propped up by the silver trade.
However, it's reliance on silver would eventually spell its doom - it would solely import silver, which is not a valuable good while exporting actually valuable luxury goods.
Still, it accumulated immense wealth, and also heavily utilized gunpowder to great effect in small scale conflicts. However, it did not develop to as great an extent of other empires and colonize other nations, only holding onto Tibet as a "colony".

Qing Empire

Gunpowder

Possibly the single most important military advancement, allowed for the rise of new gunpowder empires which utilized gunpowder heavily in military conquests for expansion.
Gunpowder was imported from China into many other nations, but these nations would innovate heavily and gunpowder technology would determine the winners and losers of P2.

Gunpowder

Yasak System

The Yasak collection was a tax collection system in Russia, of tribute that amounted to basically fur taxation.
This taxation policy was created because of the fur trade from Russia to Europe - without the fur tribute, Russia couldn’t have enough fur to trade with Western Europe for goods that they needed.
By constructing a stable taxation system, similar to the Qing single whip system, it kept the economy more stable and helped create a "unified currency" for taxation, helping the Russian Empire maintain stronger economic control over their empire.

Yasak System

Mansabdars

The Mansabdars were an elite that was granted tax collection rights by the Mughal king, similar to lesser nobles in the European structure, where these nobles also had to support a military for the Mughal emperors. However, these titles could be taken away, and were not inherited.
This allowed for direct rule by the ruler, and this direct control limited rebellion and promoted stability by limiting the power of the aristocracy.
However, this system would still end up backfiring as the Mansabdars would build up immense power and wealth leading to splinter factions breaking up the Mughal empire.

Mansabdars

Table of Ranks

The Table of Ranks was a meritocratic system in which service to the Tsar and ability/skills were theoretically the only way in which to climb the ranks. This meant that money/influence in the empire couldn’t get unskilled family members into powerful positions. However, it was slowly undermined later on, as Tsars made the requirements more lenient.
This helped the Tsar maintain their power easily as noble uprisings were more difficult as the Tsar maintained direct control of the social/political structure. Thus, this also allowed the Tsar to gain a highly skilled administrative structure.
This allowed the Russian Empire to have a lot of skilled governors/leaders/bureaucrats to administrate throughout the empire, and it also centralized power, as aristocrats/noble families couldn’t leverage their power as much if it was directly granted from the Tsar.

Table of Ranks

Devisherme

A system in which young Christian boys were forcibly converted to become Janissaries (converted to become Muslims, and members of the officer class). They could become the elites of Ottoman society, and were a critical part of Ottoman administration.
By having a class of people who were indoctrinated in loyalty to the Ottoman empire, who had no other loyalties, the Ottoman empire had a strong system of elites who were able to administrate and manage their empire efficiently without falling victim to power struggles. Furthermore, they were promoted mainly on meritocratic basis, as birth had no effect. Similar to the Mansabdars, this system helped create a meritocracy and helped the ruler maintain direct rule.

Devisherme

Mughal Empire

They would defeat the Delhi sultanate in the Battle of Panipat, and were especially famous for their religious tolerance. Under the rule of religiously tolerant leaders such as Akbar the Great, they would develop new religions such as Sikhism, but their final rulers such as Aurangzeb would be religiously intolerant and lead to the fall of the Mughals. They were important culturally, leading to Muslim rule in Hindu India and thus the aforementioned syncretism, and created pieces of monumental architecture such as the Taj Mahal.

Mughal Empire

Russian Empire

Russia during this time period was weakened by internal strife and the continued influence of the Mongols, but would begin attempts at Westernization and modernization during P2 which was somewhat successful for the elites, such as the Table of Ranks. Especially under the reign of Peter the Great, who created St. Petersburg as a "Window to the West", and aimed to westernize, some reforms were attempted.
However, the overall social system remained the same with serfdom being a core part of the economy of Russia. The lack of modernization and westernization for most of Russia made Russia unable to westernize and industrialize in P3.

Safavid Empire

Mainly notable for the fact that they are the only Shi'a gunpowder empire in Period 2, where every other nation is Sunni.
They were extremely similar to the other Muslim empires, utilizing many of the same strategies for administration, military, and economic control. The only major difference is their Shi'ism, which caused them to continuously be in conflict with the other Sunni nations around them and also led to religious intolerance.

Safavid Empire

Akbar the Great

One of the greatest Mughal kings, Akbar the Great was extremely religiously tolerant, with a policy of sulh i kul (no, you don't need to know that) a belief system/way of life of acceptance of religions. Some notable law changes were that Shia/Sunni were allowed to both worship in the same mosques, and the abolishment of the Jizya tax completely.
By promoting religious diversity, Akbar limited rebellions due to the majority Hindu population in the Mughal empire, which would have rose up in rebellion under religious limitations from foreigners. These policies led to India becoming a religious hotspot, allowing for religions to mix and spread, such as Sikhism, a merger between Hinduism and Muslim ideas. Without religious tolerance during this period, these cultures would have been unable to mix.

Akbar the Great

Millet System

A system where the Ottomans allowed subcommunities to self-govern, such as the Jews, Armenians, and Christians. They allowed them to govern themselves internally, effectively acting as a government within a government.
This religious tolerance meant that when the Ottomans conquered a region, instead of wasting time putting down uprisings due to an unwilling population, they could continue expanding rapidly. This allowed them to expand the reach of their territory tremendously and lowered the risk of rebellions internally from conquered peoples.
The Millet system allowed for many other nation-states to join the Ottoman empire and allowed them to quickly garner support from conquered nations. This led to extremely stable rule in the Ottoman empire, making the Ottoman empire one of the most stable and prosperous land-based empires, developing into the “Ottoman threat” to Europe.

Millet System

A monopoly on black pepper by the Portuguese, it was one of the major monopolies of the IOB similar to nutmeg and the Dutch.
This is a huge change: before, IOB trade was unrestricted and followed the concept of “free trade”, with independent trade-based city-states participating in trade. Monopolies limited and hindered free trade, and changed the I.O.B by introducing economic limitations.
This leads to the Portuguese being able to become extremely rich and wealthy and profit off of the pepper trade, helping them catch up to China and the empires in the Middle East.

Cartaz

The trading post empire of the Portuguese was how they “colonized” and controlled the Indian Ocean Basin trade. By forcing ships to purchase a Cartaz, a document stating they were “allowed to trade”, they made money off of the I.O.B. Without one, ships couldn’t trade at their trading ports, and the Portuguese would act as privateers and raid ships.
This system would slightly decrease trade in the I.O.B and changed the I.O.B from a "free-trade" system into a more controlled one. However, trade still continued, just with the Portuguese leeching off of its wealth.

Cartaz

Joint Stock Companies

Joint stock companies allowed for more risks to be taken across trade journeys due to distributed capital reducing the risk. Two major ones were created during P2, the British and the Dutch:

VOC

The VOC and Dutch West Indies Co. gained enough capital to field a military and were able to drive away European competitors in the area like the British. They took over the Spice Islands, especially the Banda islands (due to nutmeg, and caused a genocide there). They leveraged their wealth to essentially operate as an independent nation, where they operated without any oversight.
Due to their profit-seeking nature, they would commit multiple crimes against humanity and use their immense wealth to control the Spice islands, esp. Nutmeg, for most of this period.
Their monopoly over spice was unseen before in the IOB, and would lead to other nations wanting in like the British which would try to take India. However there was a change from previous ideals - instead of shipping nutmeg straight back to Europe, they would sell within the IOB itself for valuable goods first, starting to participate in the lucrative IOB trade which is  a change as previously mercantilist policies led to colonies always shipping back to Europe.

British East India Company

The British East India Co. was founded by the British in P.2 BEFORE the Dutch, and they would leverage their wealth to try and take over India. Using their immense wealth and status as a corporation without any state leverage, they quickly made alliances with people against the Mughals and then betrayed them once the Mughal threat was over.
They would then colonize and rule over India, chasing profit ruthlessly through activities such as growing opium, and building multiple trade monopolies and controlling the majority of the shipping back to Britain of luxury Indian and Chinese goods. (also basic commodities like saltpeter).

Gold, God, Glory

These were the main motivations for Europeans to engage in maritime trade and develop new maritime empires.

Gold:

Gain money by bypassing Italian and Ottoman middlemen on land-based trade routes, especially for Chinese luxury goods which were still in high demand. Furthermore, find colonies for cash crops/silver/gold, which would also increase the wealth of these new empires.

God:

The failure of the crusades and the rise of Muslim empires threatened Christian nation-states and the church, which slowly had less money from tithes due to reduced population believing in the Catholic Church. Furthermore, the Protestant revolution severely weakened the Catholic Church, and Catholic states such as Spain were eager to find new targets to convert to Christianity.

Glory:

With Europe finally become more stabilized and the rise of stronger nation-states that had more direct political control over their governments and nobles, these rising nation states competed against each other for power. Having more colonies meant having more resources and more territory, which helped in the competition between these states

The creation of new social structures in order to justify the Spanish use of the indigenous population, it was promoted heavily by the Spanish settlers.
It was extremely detailed and government structured, with more and more indigenous blood being classified as lower class, and provided a racist and social justification for abuse of workers.
As the colonies became more diverse, especially with African slaves, the social structure became increasingly more complex in order to continue supporting the whites/Spanish at the top of the social hierarchy.
This system was created mainly in part due to the outnumbered Spanish: at 20% of the population, they were outnumbered and needed this clear social structure to limit the power of other groups and to prevent potential uprisings/rebellions if they gained too much power.

Transatlantic Trade

The very existence of transatlantic trade was a huge change, with two major effects - the Columbian exchange and the Atlantic slave trade

Columbian exchange

The columbian exchange was an exchange of foods and other species between Afro-Eurasia and the Americas, and was a biological exchange that occurred along with trade and voyages. The impact on Afro-Eurasia was a lot of food and crops that became commonplace - these crops could also grow in Afro-Eurasia, e.g potatoes in Ireland which became extremely commonplace and a huge source of daily calories (cacao is another example that becomes commonplace later.) 
There are two key impacts on the Americas: first, the Great Dying, killing of the native populations leaving them ripe for colonization. Second, the importation of cash crops to the Americas led to the colonies becoming profitable and leading to a vicious cycle, and caused the rise of plantations and exploitation of the Americas due to cash crops being able to be grown there well.

Atlantic Slave Trade

The slave trade also began with the rise of plantations which demanded cash crops. The plantation structure was created by the Arabs to farm sugar, and was copied by Europeans on any production economy that they colonized and created.
This had a huge change on Africa, which was a major source of slaves - African nations would become funded and financed solely based on the slave trade, and even peaceful nations needed to participate (some exceptions) in order to get access to European arms to defend themselves. This left them high and dry when the Europeans abolished slavery and contributed to the long-term issues in Africa we see today.
